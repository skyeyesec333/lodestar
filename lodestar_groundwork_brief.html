01 — Context

The EXIM project finance universe, 2025–2026

US EXIM Bank is authorized through December 31, 2026, carrying $135B in aggregate exposure authority. Its "Make More in America" initiative, Project Vault ($10B+ in critical minerals), and re-opened coal/LNG financing signal an aggressive posture. The pipeline is enormous — and structurally complex.

For a project to qualify, it must be greenfield, use American EPCs and >51% US content, have a creditworthy off-taker, show 15%+ sponsor equity, and deliver a full feasibility study, business plan, and market study — all before EXIM issues even a Letter of Interest. Every document must be in "substantially final form." There are no shortcuts.

The project owner — the sponsor sitting at the center — must simultaneously court EPCs, negotiate with off-takers, manage legal counsel, court co-lenders, brief government stakeholders, and produce a coordinated data room. No mainstream project management tool is built for this reality.

02 — Pain Landscape

Top challenges facing infrastructure project owners, 2025–2026

Based on current industry research and EXIM requirements, these are the defining friction points from the project owner's chair — not the EPC's, not the lender's.

01 / CRITICAL

Stakeholder orchestration at impossible scale

A single EXIM project involves sponsors, EPCs (must be American), off-takers, local commercial bank obligors, EXIM staff, legal advisors, technical consultants, environmental reviewers, and community reps. Each runs on a different cadence. No single tool tracks all commitments, open items, and meeting outputs across this web — so things fall through.

02 / CRITICAL

"Data room readiness" is an invisible crisis

EXIM will only review "well-developed proposals" in substantially final form — and it charges project sponsors to fund its own consultant review. Owners rarely know their true readiness state until they submit and get rejected. There is no tool that maps required document artifacts to completion status and flags gaps before submission.

03 / CRITICAL

No single source of truth for meeting intelligence

Development-stage projects run dozens of discovery, negotiation, and technical meetings per month. Commitments made by EPCs in meeting 3 are forgotten by meeting 9. Without a system that captures decisions, extracts action items, and links them to the project record, the owner operates on memory and scattered email threads.

04 / HIGH

EPC selection and qualification is tribal knowledge

EXIM requires American EPCs with demonstrated capability. Evaluating, qualifying, and comparing EPC bids — especially across technical, financial, and US-content criteria — is done in Excel or not at all. There is no structured EPC pipeline management tool for project owners.

05 / HIGH

Supply chain timing risk is unmodeled

Transformer lead times now average 128 weeks. Switchgear is 44 weeks. These constraints must be baked into project schedules and financing timelines — including EXIM's Interest During Construction calculations. Most owners are modeling this in Excel with stale assumptions.

06 / HIGH

The feasibility-to-financing gap is unmapped

Feasibility studies, business plans, and market studies are typically produced by different firms on different schedules. The project owner must harmonize them into a single coherent narrative for EXIM — with consistent numbers, assumptions, and risk language. There is no tool that manages this synthesis.

07 / MEDIUM

Stakeholder engagement is under-formalized

Research shows that fewer than 1 in 31 infrastructure projects conducts a formal stakeholder analysis. For EXIM purposes, sponsors must document off-taker creditworthiness, local bank relationships, and community engagement — yet most owners still run these on experience and relationship memory.

08 / MEDIUM

Contracting model volatility

Fixed-price EPC contracts are giving way to open-book, progressive design-build, and pain/gain share. Project owners must now model multiple contracting scenarios and their impact on EXIM's lien position, cash flow coverage, and political risk coverage — a scenario planning problem no current tool addresses.

05 — Product Architecture

Groundwork — how the killer app is designed

The insight is simple but under-exploited: infrastructure projects moving toward government-backed financing have a known, structured checklist of requirements. The app's core job is to make that checklist the organizing spine of everything — meetings, stakeholders, documents, timelines, and funding relationships — all in one place.

Module
What it does
Release
EXIM Readiness Scorecard
Maps every EXIM project finance requirement (from EXIM.gov's requirements list) to a live completion status. Tracks document form (draft / substantially final / executed), flags conditions precedent, and shows a submission readiness %. AI flags narrative inconsistencies across documents.
MVP
Meeting Intelligence Layer
Connect to Zoom, Teams, or upload transcripts. AI extracts: decisions made, commitments by party, open questions, and action items. Each item is automatically linked to a stakeholder record and a project milestone. No more lost meeting notes.
MVP
Stakeholder Graph
Models all parties by type (Sponsor, EPC, Off-taker, Local Bank Obligor, Legal Counsel, EXIM Staff, Environmental Reviewer). Tracks contractual relationships, engagement status, open requirements, and documents owed by each party. Alerts when a stakeholder goes dark.
MVP
EPC Qualification Tracker
Structured pipeline for evaluating American EPC firms. Tracks US content % by scope area, capability verification status, financial standing, bid submission, and side-by-side comparison against EXIM's supplier requirements. Integrates with the EXIM Readiness Scorecard.
MVP
Funder Relationship Hub
CRM-style pipeline for EXIM, DFI co-lenders, commercial banks, and equity. Tracks engagement stage, open requirements, conditions precedent status, and required documentation per funder. Flags when a funder's condition is blocking a milestone.
MVP
Integrated Data Room
Document repository organized by EXIM's required categories (not generic folders). AI checks for: missing documents, document versions not in substantially final form, inconsistent financial figures across files, and misaligned risk language. One-click submission package generation.
V2
Supply Chain Timeline Model
Pulls live equipment lead times (transformers, switchgear, turbines) and overlays them on the project schedule. Calculates impact on Financial Close date and EXIM's Interest During Construction estimate. Flags when lead times threaten the financing timeline.
V2
Off-taker Creditworthiness Pipeline
Tracks off-taker qualification per EXIM's "reasonable assurance of repayment" standard. Manages financial statement collection, credit reference status, contract form progression, and off-taker commitment milestones. Alerts when the off-taker side risks the LOI.
V2
Contracting Scenario Modeler
Models fixed-price vs open-book vs pain/gain share EPC contracts against EXIM's security requirements. Shows how each scenario affects CAPEX predictability, lender recourse, and the project's political risk coverage eligibility. Generates a recommendation narrative for the business plan.
V3
Country & Political Risk Dashboard
Integrates EXIM's Country Limitation Schedule with project-specific political risk factors. Helps the project owner select between comprehensive and political-only coverage, documents the rationale, and tracks Schedule amendments that affect the project country.

06 — Build Strategy

What AI tools to use, and how

This is not a product you code with generic tooling. The domain complexity — EXIM regulations, multi-stakeholder contract logic, document consistency checking — requires AI at multiple layers of the stack. Here is the right toolchain:

Primary
Planning & architecture

Claude Opus via API

Use Opus for deep domain analysis: ingesting EXIM requirements documents, reasoning across complex multi-party contract structures, generating cross-document consistency checks, and producing the EXIM Readiness Scorecard logic. It handles the multi-document, multi-stakeholder reasoning that Sonnet can't sustain at depth.

Primary
Feature development

Claude Code (Planning Mode)

Use Claude Code in planning mode to map out the full data model before writing a line of code. Have it diagram the stakeholder graph schema, the document state machine (draft → substantially final → executed), and the EXIM requirements checklist taxonomy. Planning mode prevents expensive rework.

Real-time features

Claude Sonnet 4.6 (in-app)

Use Sonnet for user-facing AI features: meeting transcript processing, action item extraction, document gap flagging, and stakeholder engagement summaries. Fast, cost-effective, and sufficient for single-document and single-meeting analysis tasks.

Document intelligence

Claude API with extended context

For cross-document consistency checking (comparing assumptions across the feasibility study, business plan, and market study), use long-context API calls with all three documents loaded simultaneously. This is the feature competitors cannot replicate with standard tools.

Workflow automation

Google Antigravity / agent layer

For background tasks: monitoring EXIM's Country Limitation Schedule for changes that affect a project's country, pulling equipment lead time data, and sending stakeholder engagement alerts. An agentic layer runs these without user prompting.

Iteration speed

VS Code + Claude Code

For debugging and iterating on the data model as requirements emerge from early pilot users. The SSH-connected workflow allows rapid schema changes without breaking the live data room module. Run parallel branches for each new EXIM program requirement.

Recommended build sequence

Phase 0 (Weeks 1–4): Use Claude Opus to ingest and structure all EXIM project finance requirements into a machine-readable checklist taxonomy. This becomes the app's spine — every feature touches it.

Phase 1 (Weeks 5–12): Use Claude Code in planning mode to design the full data model — stakeholder graph, document state machine, milestone schema, meeting record structure. Get one infrastructure project owner to validate the model before coding begins.

Phase 2 (Weeks 13–24): Build the MVP five modules (Readiness Scorecard, Meeting Intelligence, Stakeholder Graph, EPC Tracker, Funder Hub). Use Sonnet 4.6 for in-app AI. Ship to 2–3 pilot project owners in the EXIM pipeline.

Phase 3 (Months 7–12): Add the Data Room and Supply Chain modules based on pilot feedback. Build the EXIM API integration (Country Limitation Schedule feeds). Begin pricing and go-to-market.

08 — Ideal Customer Profile

Who really pays, and what they pay

The paying customer is not the EPC or the bank. It is the project owner / sponsor and the advisors who live and die by deal readiness. These are people who spend months or years shepherding a single deal — and for whom a failed LOI submission is catastrophic.

Tier 1 — Primary

Independent Infrastructure Developers

$2,400–$6,000 / project / year

Firms developing 1–5 projects simultaneously in power, water, transport, or industrial sectors targeting EXIM or DFI financing. High pain, high willingness to pay. Size: 5–50 people. Decision maker: founder or VP Development.

Tier 1 — Primary

Project Finance Advisors / Advisory Boutiques

$1,500–$4,000 / project / month

Boutique advisory firms (5–30 staff) that manage the EXIM process for sponsors. They run multiple deals simultaneously and are the most natural power users. Will pay for multi-project seat pricing. Referral channel to sponsors.

Tier 2 — Expansion

US EPC Firms (Business Development)

$800–$2,000 / seat / year

Large American EPCs (required for EXIM qualification) want visibility into project pipelines where they are being considered. A Groundwork EPC seat lets them track their qualification status, respond to owner requests, and see their US content documentation.

Tier 2 — Expansion

DFI / Export Credit Agencies

Enterprise / custom

DFC, OPIC successors, and co-lending ECAs want deal visibility earlier in the pipeline. A Groundwork institutional seat gives them read access to sponsored data rooms and pipeline health scores. Long sales cycle but very high contract value.

Tier 3 — Ecosystem

Legal & Technical Consultants

$200–$600 / seat / month

Law firms (project finance practices) and technical consultants hired by sponsors. They need structured access to the data room, a way to submit documents, and visibility into open items assigned to them. Seat pricing, invited by the sponsor.

Tier 3 — Ecosystem

Sovereign & Public Sponsors

$10K–$50K / project

Government agencies or state-owned enterprises in eligible countries developing projects for EXIM support. High-value but long procurement cycles. Market after product-market fit is proven with private sponsors.
