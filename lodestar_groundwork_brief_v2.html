01 — Context

The EXIM project finance universe, 2025–2026

EXIM's active surface area is broader than the old “emerging-market power plant” stereotype. The Make More in America initiative extends EXIM support to export-oriented domestic manufacturing, with priority for small business, environmentally beneficial projects, and transformational export areas. On February 2, 2026, EXIM also approved up to $10 billion for Project Vault, making critical-minerals and supply-chain security a flagship financing theme.

That matters because the addressable workflow is no longer just classic overseas project finance. It now includes export-linked industrial capacity, strategic materials, and allied-ECA pathways where policy fit is part of the deal itself. EXIM's critical-minerals program even includes a Single Point of Entry with Export Finance Australia plus additional CTEP flexibilities, which means sponsors increasingly have to coordinate across multiple institutions and program rules at once.

The underwriting burden is still brutally manual. EXIM's own project-finance guidance centers on integrated contractual arrangements, substantially final agreements, host-government approvals, and sponsor-funded external diligence across technical, market, legal, financial, environmental, and insurance workstreams. No mainstream system is designed around that pre-close coordination load.

Working thesis: the growth area is not generic "infrastructure software." It is sponsor-side operating software for export-finance readiness, where multi-party commitments, document quality, policy fit, and funder conditions must be orchestrated before financial close.

03 — Pain Landscape

Top challenges facing infrastructure project owners, 2025–2026

Based on current EXIM guidance, 2025–2026 transaction signals, and the feature direction of adjacent SaaS, these are the defining friction points from the project owner's chair — not the EPC's, not the lender's.

01 / CRITICAL

Stakeholder orchestration at impossible scale

A live EXIM-track deal can now involve sponsors, U.S. EPCs, off-takers, local bank obligors, EXIM staff, allied ECAs, legal counsel, technical advisors, environmental reviewers, and government counterparts. Each runs on a different cadence. No single system tracks commitments, required evidence, and meeting outputs across this web, so the sponsor becomes the human API.

02 / CRITICAL

"Data room readiness" is an invisible crisis

EXIM reviews well-developed proposals, funds outside diligence through sponsor-paid reviews, and expects coordinated documentation across technical, market, legal, and financing workstreams. Owners still have no system that turns this into an evidence-backed readiness score. They learn their true state only when diligence opens or an LOI package stalls.

03 / CRITICAL

Strategic-program fit is now part of execution

For critical minerals, domestic manufacturing, and allied supply-chain cases, the sponsor must now evidence U.S. content, export nexus, engineering scope, and program fit across MMIA / CTEP / partner-ECA structures. That is not just policy narrative. It is another operating layer with real artifacts, owners, and deadlines.

04 / HIGH

No single source of truth for meeting intelligence

Development-stage projects run dozens of discovery, negotiation, and technical meetings per month. Commitments made by EPCs in meeting 3 are forgotten by meeting 9. AI note tools can summarize calls, but they still do not create a durable project memory linked to stakeholders, milestones, and funding conditions.

05 / HIGH

EPC selection and qualification is tribal knowledge

EXIM requires American EPCs with demonstrated capability. Evaluating, qualifying, and comparing EPC bids — especially across technical, financial, and US-content criteria — is done in Excel or not at all. There is no structured EPC pipeline management tool for project owners.

06 / HIGH

The feasibility-to-financing narrative is unmapped

Feasibility studies, business plans, market studies, and engineering packages are still produced by different firms on different schedules. The sponsor must harmonize them into one coherent story for EXIM and co-lenders, with consistent assumptions, figures, and risk language. No credible product owns that synthesis problem.

07 / HIGH

Construction risk and financing readiness still live in different systems

Capital-project platforms are getting better at execution planning, but they are still delivery-centric. They do not bridge pre-close sponsor work such as off-take progress, EPC qualification, lender conditions, U.S.-content evidence, and readiness-to-submit logic. The most dangerous handoff still happens in spreadsheets.

08 / MEDIUM

Supply chain timing and export evidence are still hand-built

Equipment lead times, country-of-origin data, and export-linked manufacturing evidence now matter both to schedule realism and financing logic. Owners still model this in Excel, with stale assumptions and weak traceability back to the files a lender or agency will actually review.

09 / MEDIUM

Contracting model volatility

Fixed-price EPC contracts are giving way to open-book, progressive design-build, and pain/gain share. Project owners must now model multiple contracting scenarios and their impact on EXIM's lien position, cash flow coverage, and political risk coverage — a scenario planning problem no current tool addresses.

05 — Market Gap Analysis

Deal and diligence platforms are adding secure AI summaries, redaction, trackers, and relationship intelligence. But the market is still segmented by delivery, dealmaking, or document exchange — not by sponsor-side export-finance readiness.

2025–2026 market signal: adjacent SaaS is converging on AI inside workflow, strong auditability, and integrated records. That validates buyer appetite for a smarter operating layer. It does not solve the white space Lodestar cares about: turning a fragmented sponsor process into a lender-ready, submission-ready system of record.

08 — Build Strategy

What AI tools to use, and how

This is not a product you code with generic tooling. The domain complexity — EXIM regulations, multi-stakeholder contract logic, document consistency checking — requires AI at multiple layers of the stack. Here is the right toolchain:

Primary
Planning & architecture

Claude Opus via API

Use Opus for deep domain analysis: ingesting EXIM requirements documents, reasoning across complex multi-party contract structures, generating cross-document consistency checks, and producing the EXIM Readiness Scorecard logic. It handles the multi-document, multi-stakeholder reasoning that Sonnet can't sustain at depth.

Primary
Feature development

Claude Code (Planning Mode)

Use Claude Code in planning mode to map out the full data model before writing a line of code. Have it diagram the stakeholder graph schema, the document state machine (draft → substantially final → executed), and the EXIM requirements checklist taxonomy. Planning mode prevents expensive rework.

Real-time features

Claude Sonnet 4.6 (in-app)

Use Sonnet for user-facing AI features: meeting transcript processing, action item extraction, document gap flagging, and stakeholder engagement summaries. Fast, cost-effective, and sufficient for single-document and single-meeting analysis tasks.

Document intelligence

Claude API with extended context

For cross-document consistency checking (comparing assumptions across the feasibility study, business plan, and market study), use long-context API calls with all three documents loaded simultaneously. This is the feature competitors cannot replicate with standard tools.

Workflow automation

Google Antigravity / agent layer

For background tasks: monitoring EXIM's Country Limitation Schedule for changes that affect a project's country, pulling equipment lead time data, and sending stakeholder engagement alerts. An agentic layer runs these without user prompting.

Iteration speed

VS Code + Claude Code

For debugging and iterating on the data model as requirements emerge from early pilot users. The SSH-connected workflow allows rapid schema changes without breaking the live data room module. Run parallel branches for each new EXIM program requirement.

Recommended build sequence

Phase 0 (Weeks 1–4): Use Claude Opus to ingest and structure all EXIM project finance requirements into a machine-readable checklist taxonomy. In parallel, establish the recurring business-intelligence packet: trend signal ledger, bottleneck map, competitor matrix, and buyer value map. These become the product strategy spine around the app spine.

Phase 1 (Weeks 5–12): Use Claude Code in planning mode to design the full data model — stakeholder graph, document state machine, milestone schema, meeting record structure. Get one infrastructure project owner to validate the model before coding begins.

Phase 2 (Weeks 13–24): Build the MVP five modules (Readiness Scorecard, Meeting Intelligence, Stakeholder Graph, EPC Tracker, Funder Hub). Use Sonnet 4.6 for in-app AI. Ship to 2–3 pilot project owners in the EXIM pipeline.

Phase 3 (Months 7–12): Add the Data Room and Supply Chain modules based on pilot feedback. Build the EXIM API integration (Country Limitation Schedule feeds). Begin pricing and go-to-market.

10 — Ideal Customer Profile

Who really pays, and what they pay

The paying customer is not the EPC or the bank. It is the project owner / sponsor and the advisors who live and die by deal readiness. These are people who spend months or years shepherding a single deal — and for whom a failed LOI submission is catastrophic.

Tier 1 — Primary

Independent Infrastructure Developers

$2,400–$6,000 / project / year

Firms developing 1–5 projects simultaneously in power, water, transport, or industrial sectors targeting EXIM or DFI financing. High pain, high willingness to pay. Size: 5–50 people. Decision maker: founder or VP Development.

Tier 1 — Primary

Project Finance Advisors / Advisory Boutiques

$1,500–$4,000 / project / month

Boutique advisory firms (5–30 staff) that manage the EXIM process for sponsors. They run multiple deals simultaneously and are the most natural power users. Will pay for multi-project seat pricing. Referral channel to sponsors.

Tier 2 — Expansion

US EPC Firms (Business Development)

$800–$2,000 / seat / year

Large American EPCs (required for EXIM qualification) want visibility into project pipelines where they are being considered. A Lodestar EPC seat lets them track their qualification status, respond to owner requests, and see their US content documentation.

Tier 2 — Expansion

DFI / Export Credit Agencies

Enterprise / custom

DFC, OPIC successors, and co-lending ECAs want deal visibility earlier in the pipeline. A Lodestar institutional seat gives them read access to sponsored data rooms and pipeline health scores. Long sales cycle but very high contract value.

Tier 3 — Ecosystem

Legal & Technical Consultants

$200–$600 / seat / month

Law firms (project finance practices) and technical consultants hired by sponsors. They need structured access to the data room, a way to submit documents, and visibility into open items assigned to them. Seat pricing, invited by the sponsor.

Tier 3 — Ecosystem

Sovereign & Public Sponsors

$10K–$50K / project

Government agencies or state-owned enterprises in eligible countries developing projects for EXIM support. High-value but long procurement cycles. Market after product-market fit is proven with private sponsors.
